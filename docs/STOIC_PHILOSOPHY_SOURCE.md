## The Four Cardinal Virtues

### Wisdom (Sophia/Phronesis)
The ability to distinguish good (virtue) from bad (vice) from indifferent (everything else). The knowledge that produces happiness through correct judgment.

**Subdivisions:**
- Good sense (common sense)
- Good calculation (sound reasoning)
- Quick-wittedness (mental agility)
- Discretion (judicious decision-making)
- Resourcefulness (practical problem-solving)

### Courage (Andreia)
The state of the soul unmoved by fear; self-restraint about what is fearful and terrible. Not absence of fear but acting despite fear.

**Epictetus, Discourses 2.1:**
> "Difficulties are things that show what men are. For the future, in case of any difficulty, remember that God, like a gymnastic trainer, has pitted you against a rough antagonist."

**Subdivisions:**
- Endurance (persistence through difficulty)
- Confidence (trust in capabilities)
- High-mindedness (nobility of purpose)
- Cheerfulness (positive outlook despite adversity)
- Industriousness (diligent application)

### Justice (Dikaiosyne)
The duty to fellow beings: do no harm, do good, remain steadfast and trustworthy. Marcus Aurelius called it "the source of all the other virtues."

**Marcus Aurelius, Meditations 9.1:**
> "Injustice is a kind of blasphemy. Nature designed rational beings for each other's sake: to help—not harm—one another."

**Subdivisions:**
- Piety (proper reverence)
- Honesty (truthfulness)
- Equity (fairness)
- Fair dealing (just transactions)

### Temperance (Sophrosyne)
Moderation and self-restraint; the disposition judging what should and should not be done.

**Marcus Aurelius, Meditations 4.3:**
> "If you are distressed by anything external, the pain is not due to the thing itself, but to your estimate of it; and this you have the power to revoke at any moment."

**Subdivisions:**
- Good discipline (ordered habits)
- Seemliness (appropriate conduct)
- Modesty (proper humility)
- Self-control (mastery over desires)

## Key Stoic Concepts

### Prohairesis (The Ruling Faculty)
The faculty of moral choice that distinguishes humans. It is the one thing fully in our power—our will, our assent, our judgments. Nothing external can compel prohairesis; only we can surrender it through our own choices.

**Epictetus, Discourses 1.1.23:**
> "You may fetter my leg, but my will (prohairesis) not even Zeus himself can overpower."

**Epictetus, Discourses 3.1.40:**
> "For you yourself are neither flesh nor hair, but choice (prohairesis)."

### Oikeiosis (Circles of Concern)
The natural process of extending concern from self outward to all humanity. Hierocles described concentric circles from self → family → community → humanity.

**Hierocles:**
> "It is the task of a well-tempered man, in his proper treatment of each group, to draw the circles together somehow towards the center."

### Sympatheia (Cosmic Interconnection)
The universal connection between all things. The cosmos is a single living organism whose parts are inherently linked through the omnipresent logos.

**Marcus Aurelius, Meditations 6.54:**
> "What injures the hive injures the bee."

**Marcus Aurelius, Meditations:**
> "Everything is interwoven, and the web is holy; none of its parts are unconnected."

### Apatheia (Freedom from Destructive Passions)
Apatheia means freedom from DESTRUCTIVE passions (pathē), NOT absence of all emotion. Better translated as "equanimity" than "indifference."

**What Apatheia IS:**
- Freedom from rage, envy, uncontrolled fear, excessive grief
- Emotional stability grounded in correct judgment
- Experiencing appropriate emotions (eupatheiai) while free from inappropriate ones

**What Apatheia IS NOT:**
- Emotionlessness
- Suppressing all feeling
- Being "cold" or "robotic"
- Indifference to life or others

### Eudaimonia (Flourishing)
The Stoic goal (telos)—flourishing, well-being, the good life. It is achieved through virtue alone and is unaffected by external circumstances.

**Three Aspects:**
1. Conforming to cosmic nature (accepting fate, providence)
2. Living according to human nature (reason)
3. Perfecting one's rational faculty (virtue)

## Stoic Psychology of Emotions

### Pathē (Passions) - Harmful Emotions
"Excessive impulses disobedient to reason" or "irrational contractions/expansions of the soul." Passions arise from false judgments—they are not irrational drives but errors in reasoning.

| | Present Object | Future Object |
|---|---|---|
| **Judged (wrongly) Good** | Pleasure (hēdonē) | Appetite (epithumia) |
| **Judged (wrongly) Bad** | Distress (lupē) | Fear (phobos) |

### Eupatheiai (Good Emotions) - Healthy Emotions
Rational, appropriate affective states the sage experiences. Stoicism is NOT about eliminating emotion but about experiencing correct emotions based on accurate judgments.

| | Present Object | Future Object |
|---|---|---|
| **Rationally Judged Good** | Joy (chara) | Wish (boulēsis) |
| **Rationally Judged Bad** | — | Caution (eulabeia) |

**Joy (chara):** Reasonable elation, enjoyment, good spirits, tranquility.
**Wish (boulēsis):** Reasonable striving toward future good, good will, kindliness.
**Caution (eulabeia):** Reasonable avoidance of genuine evils (vice), respect, sanctity.

### Propatheiai (Pre-Emotions)
Involuntary, reflex-like affective reactions that precede cognitive judgment—the initial physical/emotional response before reason engages.

**Examples:** Blanching at sudden danger, blushing, trembling, racing heart

**Stoic View:** These are NOT vices—even the sage experiences them. What matters is immediate control and correct subsequent judgment. The pre-emotion is automatic; the response you choose is not.

## Common Misconceptions

### "Stoicism = Don't Feel"
**Truth:** Apatheia means freedom from destructive passions, not all emotion. Stoics experience and value: joy, goodwill, compassion, love, appropriate caution. Marcus Aurelius: "To be free of passion and yet full of love."

### "Stoicism = Passive Acceptance"
**Truth:** Stoicism is a philosophy of ACTION. Marcus Aurelius was Emperor—actively ruling, leading armies. The distinction: Accept what you CANNOT change (externals), actively work on what you CAN change (your responses, efforts, character).

### "Stoicism = Toxic Positivity"
**Truth:** Stoicism is NOT "think positively about everything." It's about seeing things AS THEY ARE—clear-eyed realism. Stoics practiced negative visualization (imagining difficulties). The goal is groundedness and acceptance, not forced optimism.

### "Everything Happens for a Reason"
**Stoic Correction:** Stoics don't believe that. They believe: this happened, now what can you make of it? The meaning is what you create, not what's predetermined.

## The Three Disciplines (Epictetus)

### Discipline of Desire (Orexis)
**Focus:** What to aim for; aligning desires with nature
**Goal:** Desire only what is in your control; accept what happens
**Practice:** Want only what depends on you. Accept external outcomes as fate.

### Discipline of Action (Hormē)
**Focus:** How to behave toward others; fulfilling duties
**Goal:** Act for the common good while accepting outcomes
**Practice:** Fulfill your role. Act appropriately toward all. Work for collective good.

### Discipline of Assent (Sunkatathesis)
**Focus:** How to judge impressions; avoiding being carried away
**Goal:** Give assent only to well-examined impressions
**Practice:** Pause at impressions. Question automatic thoughts. Accept only what examination confirms.

## Modern Psychology Validation

### CBT-Stoicism Connection
Albert Ellis explicitly credited Stoicism as the foundation of REBT. "Men are disturbed not by things, but by the views which they take of them" (Epictetus) = cognitive restructuring.

**Research:** Buhle et al. (2014) meta-analysis of 48 neuroimaging studies confirms cognitive reappraisal activates prefrontal cortex and modulates amygdala.

### ACT-Stoicism Connection
**Parallels:** Values clarification, cognitive defusion, psychological flexibility, acceptance
**Research:** Hayes et al. meta-analysis: psychological flexibility correlates r = .42 with diverse positive outcomes

### Locus of Control
**Research:** Rotter (1966) and subsequent studies show internal locus of control predicts better performance, well-being, less depression.
**Stoic Parallel:** Dichotomy of control = internal locus of control

### Negative Visualization
**Research:** Norem & Cantor (1986) on defensive pessimism; Meichenbaum on Stress Inoculation Training
**Finding:** Imagining negative outcomes helps anxious individuals perform equally well as optimists
